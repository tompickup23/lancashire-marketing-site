# Lancashire Tour de France Monetisation Research

Checked on `21 March 2026`.

This note is about `Lancashire County Council` and the wider Lancashire place-marketing system, not just the race itself.

The question is not simply:

- `Will the Tour bring people?`

It is:

- `How does Lancashire convert the 2027 Tour de France into actual economic value, longer stays, business growth, place-brand lift and a usable cycling legacy?`

## 1. Executive take

The cleanest conclusion is this:

- `The Tour de France is not mainly a ticketed revenue event for LCC.`
- `It is a value-capture event.`

Because the race is `free to attend`, and because the core Tour rights sit with `A.S.O.` and the official organising committee, Lancashire should not build its commercial model around selling official Tour access, official Tour marks, or official Tour merchandise.

The winning model for Lancashire is:

- `visitor spend capture`
- `overnight-stay conversion`
- `hospitality and town-centre spend`
- `local supplier capture`
- `trade and investment programming`
- `CRM and audience-data capture for repeat visits`
- `cycling-product legacy after race day`

If Lancashire treats the Tour only as a nice day of roadside spectacle, it will under-monetise the moment badly.

If Lancashire treats it as a `county-scale marketing platform with an event attached`, it can do much better.

## 3. What LCC can monetise, and what it cannot

## 3.1 What LCC can realistically monetise

- `Visitor economy uplift`
  Food, drink, accommodation, car parking, attractions, retail, transport, and ancillary spend before, during and after the stage.

- `Longer stays`
  Stage 2 falls on a `Saturday`, which Marketing Lancashire and LCC leadership have already highlighted as an opportunity to keep people in the county overnight or for a full weekend. Source: [Marketing Lancashire Tour de France 2027 news](https://www.marketinglancashire.com/news/world-class-cyclists-heading-to-lancashire-for-tour-de-france-2027/).

- `Hospitality packaging`
  Lancashire can sell weekenders, coast-and-cycling breaks, Bowland food stays, bayfront stays, and race-viewing packages through local businesses and destination partners.

- `Supplier capture`
  The official organising committee is openly signposting `procurement and contracting` opportunities. Lancashire can organise local firms so more event spending lands in-county. Source: [Grand Départ GB home page](https://www.letourgb.com/).

- `Business-to-business and inward-investment profile`
  Yorkshire used the 2014 Grand Départ to run an `International Business Festival` for more than `2,000 participants from 10 nations`, with the explicit aim of trade and investment growth. Source: [Three Inspirational Days impact summary](https://content.tfl.gov.uk/tour-de-france-three-inspirational-days-impact.pdf).

- `Data capture and future tourism demand`
  The event should be used to grow Lancashire CRM lists, social audiences, repeat-visit pipelines and bookable cycling itineraries, not just one weekend’s footfall.

- `Cycling-product legacy`
  Route signage, downloadable ride sections, family-friendly routes, cycling-friendly business schemes and short-break packaging can all create post-event value. Pendle’s own official visitor-economy work around the `Tour of Britain` is a strong local precedent. Source: [Pendle Visitor Economy Plan / Tour of Britain legacy actions](https://www.pendle.gov.uk/download/meetings/id/20547/item_19_appendix).

## 3.2 What LCC should not assume it can monetise directly

- `Ticket sales`
  There are no spectator tickets. Source: [Grand Départ GB FAQs](https://www.letourgb.com/copy-of-press-release-2).

- `Official Tour sponsorship inventory`
  Inference from the official Grand Départ site and A.S.O. rights pages: the core official commercial rights sit with the official organising committee and `A.S.O.`, not with Lancashire County Council. Sources: [Grand Départ GB home page](https://www.letourgb.com/), [A.S.O. legal / copyright page](https://www.letour.fr/en/copyrights).

- `Official Tour branding and merch rights`
  Inference from the same rights structure: Lancashire should not assume it can freely create `official` Tour-branded product lines without the relevant permissions. Sources: [A.S.O. legal / copyright page](https://www.letour.fr/en/copyrights), [Grand Départ GB home page](https://www.letourgb.com/).

- `Headline economic-impact numbers as council income`
  The big impact figures are mostly `regional economic effects`, not cash landing directly in council accounts.

- `Local resident spend as new money`
  Yorkshire’s own evaluation is explicit that local-resident spending is real but was `not counted` in the headline external economic impact. Source: [Three Inspirational Days full report](https://democracy.york.gov.uk/%28S%28aw2b23jofoyuejfc1asnl055%29%29/documents/s95197/1.%20Background%20Paper%20-%20THREE%20INSPIRATIONAL%20DAYS%20FULL%20FINAL.pdf).

## 4. What past editions tell Lancashire

## 4.1 Britain’s Tour history

The official A.S.O. history for Britain is:

- `1974` Plymouth circuit stage
- `1994` Brighton and Portsmouth stages after the Channel Tunnel crossing
- `2007` London Grand Départ
- `2014` Yorkshire Grand Départ
- `2027` the new three-nation Grand Départ across Scotland, England and Wales

Source: [A.S.O. Grand Départ 2027 page](https://www.letour.fr/en/the-race/grands-departs/grand-depart-2027).

## 4.2 London 2007

The London Grand Départ mattered because it established the event as a UK place-marketing tool, not just a bike race.

- TfL’s objectives included `promoting cycling`, `marketing London on a world stage and encouraging tourism`, and proving that London could host major events. Source: [TfL Tour de France research summary](https://content.tfl.gov.uk/tour-de-france-research-summary.pdf).

- UK Sport said at the time that it had committed `£500,000` of Lottery funding to staging costs and that the event would contribute `millions of pounds` to the South East region. Source: [UK Sport: Tour de France departs in London](https://www.uksport.gov.uk/news/2006/02/09/tour-de-france-departs-in-london).

Main Lancashire lesson:

- use the race to prove `host credibility`, not only to chase one day of spend

## 4.3 Yorkshire 2014

Yorkshire is the strongest UK benchmark for Lancashire.

Official impact work found:

- `£128 million` total boost across Yorkshire, Cambridgeshire, Essex and London
- `£102 million` boost for Yorkshire
- `4.8 million` roadside spectators
- `over half a million` repeat visits within three months
- around `800,000` visitors strongly agreed they would recommend host areas
- an `International Business Festival` with `over 2,000 participants from 10 nations`

Sources: [Three Inspirational Days impact summary](https://content.tfl.gov.uk/tour-de-france-three-inspirational-days-impact.pdf), [Three Inspirational Days full report](https://democracy.york.gov.uk/%28S%28aw2b23jofoyuejfc1asnl055%29%29/documents/s95197/1.%20Background%20Paper%20-%20THREE%20INSPIRATIONAL%20DAYS%20FULL%20FINAL.pdf).

The same evaluation also matters for discipline:

- local resident spend was estimated at about `£48 million`, but it was `not counted` in the headline economic figures because it was not new money into the host economies
- future return visits by spectators were modelled as an additional `£43 million` regional impact beyond the immediate event

Source: [Three Inspirational Days full report](https://democracy.york.gov.uk/%28S%28aw2b23jofoyuejfc1asnl055%29%29/documents/s95197/1.%20Background%20Paper%20-%20THREE%20INSPIRATIONAL%20DAYS%20FULL%20FINAL.pdf).

Main Lancashire lessons:

- maximise `external` spend, not just local churn
- build a `return-visit` engine
- run a serious `business and trade programme` alongside the race

## 4.4 York and the cost-discipline lesson

York’s later `Tour de Yorkshire` cabinet paper is useful because it shows how a host city thought about actual public-sector delivery.

- York estimated total hosting cost at `£250,000`, including a `£100,000` host fee payable to `A.S.O.`
- the paper explicitly said York would seek `best-value` supplies independently and intended to `source locally wherever possible to keep money in the region`

Source: [City of York Council Tour de Yorkshire cabinet paper](https://democracy.york.gov.uk/documents/s94168/Tour%20de%20Yorkshire%20Cabinet%20Paper%2016.12.14.pdf).

Main Lancashire lesson:

- treat procurement capture as a monetisation lever in its own right

## 4.5 Utrecht 2015

Utrecht is useful because it shows what happens when a city treats the Tour as a full civic activation platform.

The University of Utrecht evaluation says:

- `748,000` city visits linked to the Tour start
- `370,000` unique visitors
- an `8.4` visitor rating for the Tour-start weekend
- a `100-day` activation programme with `250` activities
- `138,000` participants and `570,000` visitors in that pre-event programme
- economic impact of `€25.277 million` for Utrecht and the direct region
- national media value of `more than €33 million`
- delivered on a `€15.4 million` budget

Source: [University of Utrecht evaluation summary](https://www.uu.nl/nieuws/le-grand-depart-utrecht-2015-een-feest-voor-de-stad).

Main Lancashire lessons:

- start activation long before race day
- treat culture, communities and volunteers as part of the economic model
- measure media value and civic participation alongside spend

## 4.6 Denmark 2022

Denmark is a strong model for destination-marketing conversion around the race.

VisitDenmark’s `Detour de France` campaign used the Danish Tour start as a tourism-marketing platform across multiple source markets and reported:

- `88.7 million` total impressions
- `111.6 million` press reach
- `DKK 16.3 million` PR ad-value

VisitDenmark also says the campaign was built around a coordinated cross-market story, video-led creative, search capture, press visits, and local destination content in stage areas.

Source: [VisitDenmark Detour de France campaign case study](https://www.visitdenmark.dk/corporate/aktiviteter/detour-de-france-eng).

Main Lancashire lessons:

- own a clear `county story`
- create a national-and-international media package, not just local festivities
- connect paid media, earned media, search demand, and destination landing pages

## 4.7 Lancashire’s own smaller cycling legacy precedent

Pendle’s official visitor-economy planning after hosting a `Tour of Britain` stage is one of the most relevant local examples.

Pendle’s plan included:

- advertising to walking and cycling audiences nationally
- targeting clubs outside the immediate catchment
- publishing route sections online
- designing family-friendly route variants
- building a `cycling-friendly` tip sheet for tourism businesses
- promoting cycling-friendly accommodation and businesses more heavily

Source: [Pendle Visitor Economy Plan / Tour of Britain legacy actions](https://www.pendle.gov.uk/download/meetings/id/20547/item_19_appendix).

Main Lancashire lesson:

- the race should become a `bookable product` and a `business-readiness programme`, not just an event memory

## 5. The monetisation model Lancashire should use

## 5.1 Layer 1: race-weekend capture

This is the shortest-horizon layer.

LCC and partners should aim to increase:

- overnight stays on `Friday 2 July`, `Saturday 3 July`, and `Sunday 4 July 2027`
- breakfast, lunch, takeaway and pub spend
- parking and park-and-ride usage where appropriate
- attraction admissions before and after the stage
- spend in towns that are not exact route points but are natural bases

Best practical focus:

- `Lancaster + Morecambe + Ribble Valley + West Lancashire` weekend packaging
- route-day breakfast, picnic and takeaway offers
- after-race evening programming in town centres
- Sunday recovery itineraries so visitors do not simply leave after the stage

## 5.2 Layer 2: sponsor and partner capture

Lancashire should create `Lancashire-side` sponsorship and partner inventory that does not pretend to be official Tour inventory.

Examples:

- Lancashire live sites and fan hubs
- Lancashire food and drink villages
- Lancashire cycling fringe programme
- Lancashire business breakfasts and trade receptions
- Lancashire route guides and visitor welcome packs

Important distinction:

- `official Tour sponsorship` belongs to the official rights structure
- `Lancashire festival / welcome / fringe sponsorship` is where local monetisation can be built

This is an inference from the official A.S.O. rights structure and the official organising committee’s own sponsorship/procurement forms. Sources: [Grand Départ GB home page](https://www.letourgb.com/), [A.S.O. legal / copyright page](https://www.letour.fr/en/copyrights).

## 5.3 Layer 3: local business readiness

This is probably the most under-rated monetisation lever.

LCC should coordinate a county-wide business readiness pack covering:

- opening hours
- race-day access advice
- takeaway and fast-serve offers
- spectator-friendly toilets and facilities
- cycling-friendly credentials
- multilingual / international visitor basics
- booking conversion and CRM capture

Pendle’s official legacy work already points toward `cycling-friendly` business guidance and stronger route publishing. Source: [Pendle Visitor Economy Plan / Tour of Britain legacy actions](https://www.pendle.gov.uk/download/meetings/id/20547/item_19_appendix).

## 5.4 Layer 4: trade, investment and business events

Yorkshire’s International Business Festival is the clearest warning not to waste the B2B side of the opportunity.

Lancashire should run:

- a `Lancashire on the move` investment reception
- aerospace / advanced manufacturing / clean energy side events
- food and hospitality buyer showcases
- cycling and active-travel innovation panels
- a supplier matchmaking process for local firms

This is the right place to connect:

- `BAE Systems / FalconWorks`
- advanced manufacturing
- clean energy
- tourism and hospitality
- food production
- universities and skills

## 5.5 Layer 5: post-event cycling and repeat-visit legacy

This is where the long-tail value sits.

Lancashire should publish and promote:

- `Ride the Tour through Lancashire`
- route sections by difficulty
- family variants
- coast-to-climb itineraries
- cycling weekends with food and heritage stops
- race-viewing landmarks turned into year-round cycling product

The point is to convert a one-day global event into a `multi-year cycling proposition`.

## 7. The women’s race and why Lancashire should stay careful

As of `21 March 2026`:

- `Visit Lancashire` says the `Tour de France Femmes avec Zwift` will cross Lancashire on the way from Leeds to Manchester
- the official organising committee says the full detailed routes for `Leeds to Manchester` and `Manchester to Sheffield` will be announced in `spring 2026`

So the right current line is:

- `Lancashire should prepare to monetise the women’s race`
- but `should not over-claim exact route geography yet`

Sources: [Visit Lancashire event page](https://www.visitlancashire.com/whats-on/tour-de-france-gb-2027-p1162190), [Grand Départ GB FAQs](https://www.letourgb.com/copy-of-press-release-2).

## 8. What should go into the Lancashire marketing plan now

The Tour should sit inside the Lancashire plan as:

- `a global visibility moment`
- `a county hospitality and stay-conversion programme`
- `a business and procurement opportunity`
- `a cycling-legacy platform`

The immediate planning moves are:

1. Build a `Tour de France Lancashire commercial group` across LCC, Marketing Lancashire, Visit Lancashire, district councils, major hospitality operators and business-representation bodies.
2. Produce a `Lancashire business readiness pack` for route towns and stay hubs.
3. Create `bookable weekenders` now in principle, then swap in final route/timing details when released.
4. Build a `Lancashire fringe programme` that can carry local sponsorship without infringing official Tour rights.
5. Run a `trade and investment` programme around the race rather than leaving the B2B value on the table.
6. Capture first-party data aggressively through landing pages, itineraries, downloads and sign-ups.
7. Convert the route into a post-event `rideable Lancashire product` with maps, sections and itineraries.

## 9. Bottom line

The Tour de France will not be well monetised by Lancashire if it is handled as a one-day celebration.

It will be well monetised if Lancashire treats it as:

- `one day of race`
- `one month of programming`
- `one year of place-marketing`
- `multiple years of cycling, tourism and business legacy`
